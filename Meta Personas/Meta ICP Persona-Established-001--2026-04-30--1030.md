## Summary

Optimizon's prospect and client base across these seven accounts centres on established UK brands — spanning home goods, filtration, kitchen/bath fixtures, sportswear licensing, food & beverage, pet food, and fragrance/cosmetics — that are operating on Amazon (primarily via a Vendor/1P relationship) but are experiencing stagnation, revenue decline, or a lack of strategic direction. The common thread is that these brands have Amazon presence but feel out of control: prices are being eroded, content is weak, advertising is either absent or untargeted, and their current agency or in-house resource is too tactical and not strategic enough. The primary decision-maker or champion is typically a lone e-commerce or digital lead, often navigating internal politics, tight budgets, and sceptical finance or senior stakeholders. They are looking for a partner — not just an operator — who brings strategic thinking, data-driven decisions, and proactive communication.

**Key profile bullets:**
- UK-based brands with existing Amazon presence (most are vendor/1P, some are transitioning to or adding seller/3P)
- Revenue range on Amazon: from early-stage (Blanco: ~20 sales in 2024) to established (Assisi: £2.6M+ vendor sell-in; IBC Simply: strong UK base)
- Company size: SME to mid-market; some are subsidiaries of global groups (Brita, Aqualisa/Fortune Brands, Blanco)
- Category: premium or specialist products — typically not the cheapest option in their category
- Primary pain: loss of control (pricing, content, data), declining revenue, under-served by current agency, and lack of Amazon strategy
- Primary desire: growth, channel control, proactive strategic partnership, and better data/reporting
- Decision-making: typically involves a champion (e-commerce lead), a blocker (finance or senior management), and in some cases global group governance
- Budget: cautious — most are working within constrained or unapproved budgets; commission-based or performance-linked models are preferred over large upfront retainers

## Recommendations

### Key Patterns and Areas to Watch

**1. The "strategic gap" is the primary pitch hook.**
Every account or prospect who is unhappy with their current setup describes the same problem: their agency or internal team is operational/tactical, not strategic. Optimizon must lead with strategic thinking in discovery conversations — not just capabilities. Sales questions should probe: "Who owns the Amazon strategy today? What does that look like in practice?"

**2. Finance and internal stakeholders are the real decision-makers in many accounts.**
Jonathan Crowe (Aqualisa) had his marketing budget frozen. Michael Woodhead (IBC Simply) blocked EU expansion. Assisi's VC backers override some decisions. INCOS' internal PCOGS confusion cut ad spend. In every pitch and renewal, Optimizon should explicitly address how it helps the champion sell the value internally — scorecard reporting, clear ROI frameworks, and staged investment models all serve this purpose.

**3. Pricing and channel management is the universal pain — position the hybrid model as the solution.**
Vendor clients universally fear price erosion. The hybrid 1P+3P model — where Optimizon's seller account maintains price integrity, serves as a buy-box backup, and enables new launches — is a strong differentiator. It addresses the fear without requiring the brand to abandon their vendor relationship.

**4. Green flags to look for:**
- Brand operates in premium/specialist category (not commodity)
- Amazon revenue is declining or flat despite overall brand growth
- Current agency is described as "operational" or "tactical"
- The champion is a lone e-commerce lead or small team with upward reporting to finance/commercial
- Brand has wide retail distribution (creates price-matching pressure = motivation to control Amazon)
- EU or international expansion is on the roadmap even if not yet viable

**5. Red flags to watch:**
- Finance Director or VC is actively blocking investment (likely slow sales cycle; need champion strategy)
- Brand's product price points are below ~£10 (Amazon fees eat margin; economics hard to justify)
- No clear hard revenue targets (Blanco: no hard goals) = harder to close
- Content/range is about to undergo major change (Aqualisa's EU Drinking Water Directive) = risk of low ROI on content investment in the short term
- Global group governance sets 1P/vendor strategy and cannot be changed at UK level (Brita: 1P set by Germany)

**6. The "proactive partner" positioning resonates across all accounts.**
Multiple prospects explicitly said they want a partner who brings ideas — not one that waits to be told what to do. In sales conversations, Optimizon should demonstrate proactivity during the discovery itself: do live audits, point out issues on their Amazon listing during the call, show competitor analysis. This is what converts "curious" prospects into signed clients (as evidenced by Aqualisa's response to Juan's live listing analysis in the first call).

**7. Reporting quality is a retention and expansion issue.**
INCOS' relationship degraded when reporting failed to present the full picture (PCOGS vs retail). Assisi's VC backers scrutinise NTB and ROAS. IBC Simply's FD needs margin clarity. Building a scorecard-first reporting approach — one that addresses the finance stakeholder, not just the e-commerce champion — is critical for account retention and growth.

**8. Team continuity matters disproportionately.**
INCOS' relationship crisis was triggered by Dan Walsh stepping back. Assisi's conversation explicitly acknowledged transition risk. Brita wants to know who they'll be working with day-to-day. In new business pitches, introduce the full account team early; make handovers visible and managed.
