## Summary

This persona covers **mature, multi-decade global brands with very entrenched Amazon 1P (vendor) relationships, where group-HQ politics and sheer 1P profitability prevent moving away from vendor — but where vendor-margin squeeze, restrictive net PPM / promo terms, unauthorised resellers, and out-of-stock buy-box loss are forcing the brand to set up a Retail Model / 3P operator as a parallel "insurance policy" channel.** The unifying thesis is **NOT vendor replacement**: it is **vendor defence**. The 3P operator (Optimizon, on a seller-of-record, consignment-stocked, FBM-or-FBA model) gives the brand the ability to (a) regain the buy box when 1P is out of stock or de-listed, (b) discipline pricing without HQ visibility, (c) hold leverage in net PPM / terms negotiations with Amazon, and (d) reduce single-channel-eggs-in-one-basket risk without re-platforming the entire account. Decision-makers are senior commercial / regional / Amazon leads operating under a global head office (Stockholm for Marshall, Germany for Brita) where 1P decisions are made at group level — they need a model that gives them back operational control without forcing a HQ-level conversation about leaving 1P.

**Key profile bullets:**
- Long-established global brands (Marshall 60+ yrs of audio heritage; Brita 60+ yrs of water filtration) with multi-billion-dollar / multi-hundred-million unit footprints
- Amazon Europe is the **biggest global e-com channel** for both — Marshall ~500k units/yr, c.40-50m peacocks; Brita "huge" volumes (700-2,000 units/day on exclusive lines)
- Setup is **entrenched 1P (vendor)** — both with sophisticated internal vendor management, hotline access to the vendor manager, and 1P decisions made at group HQ level, not UK
- Pain stack: vendor net PPM creep, restrictive promo / terms negotiations, vendor out-of-stock leaving the buy box exposed, reseller and unauthorised distributor noise (cross-border Northern Ireland in Brita's case), HQ-level political constraints on changing the 1P model, single-channel concentration risk
- Both are explicitly **NOT looking to replace 1P** — Brita group view is "highly unlikely we move away from 1P in the next 5 years"; Marshall is using a hybrid 3P operator as **negotiation leverage** in the live RFP / terms-negotiation cycle with Amazon
- Decision-makers: senior regional / commercial leadership (Marshall Dan Cornelius — Regional Director, Northern Europe; Marshall Mike Ashton — Amazon Europe Lead) or UK Amazon owner transitioning to group HQ (Brita Sadie Baile-Roberts → Marc Lukawsky); group HQ in Germany / Sweden is the gatekeeper on strategic moves
- Both want **consignment / seller-of-record** with the brand retaining stock ownership (Marshall explicit) — *not* a buy-sell distributor like Pattern, because that recreates the same pricing-control problem they have with Amazon
- Both want the 3P operator to **hide** the relationship from Amazon — i.e. the seller of record is the 3P operator, not the brand, to avoid retaliation from the 1P vendor team
- Both are price-control-led, not promo-led — Brita is "always price-matching" because someone in the trade is always on promo somewhere; Marshall wants to "wean off the drug of pure deals"
- Commercial appetite: Brita open to **commission-only OR retainer + commission** structures (tiered); Marshall is RFP-stage, expects an indication on commission and setup considerations, NDA-first
- Volumes scale wildly and the operator must accept that — Marshall: "1,000 units a month to 30,000 units a month, maybe more"; Brita: 700-2,000 units/day exclusive + sale lines

## Recommendations

**1. Lead with the "1P-defence channel" framing — never the "Amazon growth" framing.**
This cohort does NOT want to grow Amazon by replacing vendor. They want to **defend** the vendor relationship by setting up a parallel 3P channel that takes pressure off net PPM negotiations and gives them buy-box recapture when 1P is out of stock or de-listed. **Open the pitch with: "Your problem is leverage. The 3P channel is your insurance policy and your negotiation lever — it never has to be your primary channel."** Use Brita as the live working case study for Marshall — Mike Ashton and Dan Cornelius both reacted positively to the live Brita reference in the discovery call.

**2. The consignment / seller-of-record / brand-retains-stock model is non-negotiable.**
Both brands explicitly will NOT engage on a buy-sell distributor model (e.g. Pattern). The model that lands is: **Optimizon is the seller of record, Optimizon is the agent / broker, the brand retains stock ownership at the 3PL, the brand retains pricing control via min/max corridors.** Marshall articulated this in their own words at [08:58] / [02:38]. Brita is operating it now via eManaged. This is the architectural commitment to make in the first 30 minutes of every meeting.

**3. The 3P operator must be invisible to Amazon vendor / 1P.**
The seller-of-record must be Optimizon (not the brand). Brita confirmed this is how eManaged operates and how they want Optimizon to operate. Marshall confirmed the requirement independently — _"not being seen by Amazon to have a third-party business which we're running directly."_ This is the political constraint that converts the model from "interesting" to "viable" for the brand.

**4. Plan for slow-adapt group HQ politics from Day 1.**
Brita has a German group HQ that is _"quite restrictive"_ (Marc's own words at [19:11]). Marshall has a Stockholm HQ and a Northern-Europe regional layer that gates strategic decisions. **Build the engagement plan around the brand's own pace — quarterly business reviews, asynchronous handovers, allow 8 weeks for what feels like a 5-minute job.** Do not force urgency that the brand cannot match internally.

**5. Build the in-person site-visit invitation into the close motion.**
Brita is doing a 3PL York site visit (Logistics Director Steve / Marc Lukawsky / Sadie). Marshall has been invited to do the same and Dan accepted on the call. **The 3PL York visit is becoming the standard "trust-establishing" close mechanism for this cohort.** Treat it as a structural step, not a courtesy.

**6. Commission-only OR retainer-plus-commission — both must be on the table.**
Brita asked explicitly for two proposals (commission-only tiered, OR retainer + smaller commission) and is taking both internally. Marshall is RFP-stage and expects a commission indication post-NDA. **Don't push one model — present both, let the brand internalise the choice. Brita's Sadie [05:31] Catchup Call: _"I haven't really looked at a figure because I really thought, I guess that's up to you guys to look at in terms of what your costs are and where you think it should be."_**

**7. Be ready for "no revenue guarantee" pushback.**
Brita explicitly refused any revenue guarantee on the trial period at [01:11] Catchup Call. The mitigation Optimizon offered was a 6-month review window. **Build the financial floor into the commercial structure (retainer or minimum commission threshold) rather than relying on guaranteed-volume language.**

**8. Customer service integration is part of the operating model.**
Brita's "Brita Care" team handles complex questions; Optimizon's pod absorbs standard queries with a knowledge-base from the brand. This is a workstream, not a footnote — bake it into the kickoff plan.

**9. Watch for personnel rotation mid-engagement.**
Brita's primary contact moves to Germany HQ in January; the relationship transfers to Marc Lukawsky (Amazon-literate, 12-yr Brita veteran). Marshall's primary single-point-of-accountability is Mike Ashton — a 15-yr Amazon veteran whose continuity is critical. **Build a Day-1 rapport with the secondary contact and pre-stage the QBR / handover cadence to absorb personnel changes.**

**10. Green flags to look for in this archetype:**
- Heritage / multi-decade global brand with sophisticated 1P operation already in place
- Group HQ in Germany / Sweden / EU country (NOT in the UK) — UK / regional decision-maker is the buyer but not the ultimate gatekeeper
- Live or recent vendor net PPM / terms negotiation cycle in flight
- Explicit framing of "insurance policy" / "redundancy" / "eggs in one basket" / "leverage"
- Explicit refusal to operate on a buy-sell distributor model
- Senior commercial / regional leadership on the call (not procurement, not junior account managers)
- Volume scale that justifies retail-model overhead (1k–30k+ units/month)
- Multi-market complexity — pan-European or "we'll start UK then move EU"
- Already-existing trust in the dual-partner / authorised-partner model (Brita has it; Marshall is RFP-stage but framing it the same way)

**11. Red flags to watch:**
- **Small project risk**: Marshall explicitly said the 3P operator may end up shipping "a thousand units a month" if vendor terms negotiations land favourably for the brand. Pricing must work at the floor.
- **Group HQ veto risk**: any of these brands can have a regional plan vetoed at HQ level. Optimizon should not over-invest in market launches that haven't cleared HQ sign-off.
- **Personnel handover mid-engagement**: Brita's Sadie-to-Marc-to-John chain creates a 3-handover sequence inside the first 6 months — onboarding documentation and meeting cadence must absorb this.
- **Existing-incumbent loyalty**: Brita is _"happy"_ with eManaged and the Optimizon allocation will start small and prove out. Do not pitch volume-displacement; pitch differentiation (DSP, Nozzle, Smolen group, multi-marketplace breadth).
- **Vendor-team retaliation risk**: if the brand is identified as the operator of the parallel 3P account, Amazon can retaliate in vendor terms negotiations. Optimizon's seller-of-record and the EIN / business identity must be Optimizon's own.
